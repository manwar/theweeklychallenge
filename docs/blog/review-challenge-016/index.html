Task #1

The Pythagoras Pie Puzzle, which was originally published in the Pythagoras Magazine and made known through the Guardian’s article.

While this is a simple question, we loved how Duncan C. White described it, “Beautifully clearly described. Sounds straightforward.”

The solution was quite straight forward but you just need to aware of long floating point numbers as shown in the output of Duane Powell’s solution.

Task #2

This was probably the hardest and demanding task of all challenges. Some participants have to rewrite existing reference solutions to fit their own coding style or ported solutions from other programming languages. Read through all the blog posts on how each participants learned, researched, discovered, and formulated their solutions for this task.

There were two groups of participants, those who used CPAN modules to decode the base58 or also known as bitcoin alphabet and those who wrote their own base58 decoder. For the CPAN modules consumer, `Crypt::Misc` (Athanasius) and `Encode::Base58::GMP` (Roger Bell West). For those who implemented their own decoder, Adam Russell, Daniel Mantovani, Laurent Rosenfeld, Yozen Hernandez, Duncan C White, Feng Chang, Steven Wilson, E. Choroba, and Joelle Maslak.

While there were many ways used to declare the base58 alphabets which excluded certain characters, we found these two distinctive methods which help to improve readability. First one by Daniel Mantovani (his solution have very well-written comments, same went to Steven Wilson and Joelle Maslak as well) and the second one by E. Choroba.

Which is your preferred way?

By Daniel Matovani

# we start by defining an array with the ordered symbols of the
# base 58 representation
# note the "holes" left to highlight the non existing characters
# (number "0", upper case letter "I", upper case letter "O", and
# lower case letter "l"

my @base58 = qw {
  1 2 3 4 5 6 7 8 9
  A B C D E F G H   J K L M N   P Q R S T U V W X Y Z
  a b c d e f g h i j k   m n o p q r s t u v w x y z
};

By E. Choroba

my @b58 = grep /[^0IOl]/, 0 .. 9, 'A' .. 'Z', 'a' .. 'z';

The unorthodox solution of the week went to Lubos Kolouch. His solution basically delegate the validation to an online web service.
